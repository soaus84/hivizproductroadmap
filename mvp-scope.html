Why this closes the loop
The field observation pipeline excels at Execute-domain factors — what frontline workers and supervisors can directly observe. The survey reaches Guide and Enable factors that are structurally invisible at field level. Together they cover all 15 factors across the Forge Works Map® with evidence from the people best positioned to answer each one.
The platform also solves the honest-answer problem. Anonymous surveys dispatched through a tool people already use for safety intelligence are more likely to produce honest responses about leadership behaviour and goal conflict than surveys known to be commissioned by leadership. The anonymisation is architectural — not a setting, not a promise.
And the output feeds directly back into the improvement pipeline. A survey that identifies a Senior Leadership factor gap doesn't just produce a report — it can surface a Critical Insight that enters the toolbox talk and enquiry pipeline. The diagnostic and the action system are the same system.

Reporting design note — revisit before building
Reports are periodic, not ad hoc. Monthly or quarterly cadence, auto-generated, landing automatically in the hands of all safety managers whose scope includes that org unit. A North Region report is a North Region document — any safety manager scoped to North Region or above can see it.

Ownership: Reports carry org_unit_type + org_unit_id, not an owner_id. generated_by is audit trail only — not access control. Scope membership determines visibility.

Discussion artefact: Reports should be designed as prompts for conversation, not just documents. A safety manager reviewing a period report should be able to enter a manual insight directly from that context — "I spotted something in this period that the algorithm didn't flag."

Notifications: Both safety managers with overlapping scope receive the notification when a report is generated. Intentional — best they discuss it. Platform doesn't decide ownership, it ensures awareness.

Scope of this epic: Currently specced as on-demand generation. Revisit the full periodic reporting design — cadence configuration, auto-distribution, discussion threading, manual insight entry from report context — as a dedicated conversation before V3 reporting is built.

Investigation Integration — Controls as Contributing Factors
When an investigation is opened, the investigator can select which critical controls were absent or failed as contributing factors. Structured attribution replaces free-text guesswork. Forge Works Map® classification on control-derived insights is significantly more precise.
Safety Manager System
▲
Control selection in investigation frameworkWhen AI assists the investigation framework (Prompt 3), it checks recent verification records for the worksite and work type. Failed or missing verifications in the period preceding the incident are surfaced as candidate contributing factors — structured, not inferred. Investigator confirms or dismisses each. investigation_contributing_control junction table links the investigation to specific control records.

Control failure → precise FW classificationA contributing control has a work_type and a failure_consequence already defined in the register. The fw_classify prompt receives this structured context alongside the narrative — significantly higher confidence classifications result. A harness anchor point control failing maps more precisely to management_systems than a free-text description of the same event ever could.
AI
Control improvement loop — corrective actions back to registerWhen an investigation identifies a control as a contributing factor, a corrective action can target the control itself — not just the incident response. "Update hot work PTW to require below-deck fire watch" becomes a proposed amendment to the global control register, reviewed by the safety team before being pushed to all sites. Investigation findings improve the register. The register improves future verifications. The loop closes.

"This control was last verified 12 days ago — result: in place. It was due for re-verification 4 days before the incident." That timeline is often the most important piece of evidence and currently has to be reconstructed manually.

Discovery Discussion Notes — For Internal Expert Consultation
The following are open questions and directional options to work through with safety and product experts before V5 is designed. Not decisions — discussion starters.
1. Register structure — work type vs MUE (Material Unwanted Event)
Current design scopes controls to work type (Hot Work, WAH etc). Industry standard (ICMM) organises controls around Material Unwanted Events — the specific fatal or catastrophic outcomes, not the work activity. A work type can have multiple MUEs; an MUE can span multiple work types.
Options to discuss:
A. Keep work-type scoping — simpler, consistent with rest of platform, sufficient for most construction and industrial contexts. Loses the bowtie rigour but avoids significant complexity.
B. Add MUE as a first-class entity above controls — controls belong to MUEs, MUEs are associated with work types. More aligned to mining CCM practice, supports bowtie-style visualisation. More complex to maintain.
C. Start with work-type scoping, allow MUE layer to be added later as an optional enhancement for mining customers.
Consult: Is your target customer base (construction, mining, industrial) using MUE/bowtie language? If mining is primary, B may be necessary. If construction is primary, A is sufficient.
2. Verification tier model — one tier or three
Industry practice uses three tiers: Tier 1 (frontline, shift-start, existence check), Tier 2 (supervisor/manager, weekly, early effectiveness), Tier 3 (safety professional/management, monthly/quarterly, full effectiveness). Current V5 design has two — supervisor daily and manager periodic.
Options to discuss:
A. Two tiers (supervisor + manager/safety professional) — simpler, sufficient for most contexts, aligns with existing platform roles.
B. Full three tiers — adds a formal independent Tier 3 owned by safety professionals with deeper effectiveness questions. More aligned to mining CCM. Could reuse the enquiry infrastructure for Tier 3.
C. Single configurable tier per control — the organisation sets who verifies and how often, no platform-defined tier structure. Maximum flexibility, minimum opinion.
Consult: Do your target organisations have formal verification tier structures today? Or is this aspirational? If aspirational, a simpler model they'll actually use beats a rigorous model they won't.
3. TARP — Trigger Action Response Plans
When a critical control is verified as not in place, what happens? Current design: notification to manager and safety manager, work should not commence, human decision. Industry standard (particularly mining) uses pre-defined TARPs — staged, unambiguous response actions defined per control, not decided ad hoc when a failure is discovered.
Options to discuss:
A. Platform-defined response: notification + work hold recommendation. Human decides. Simpler, less prescriptive, works across industries.
B. TARP attached to each control in the register: pre-defined response steps surface immediately on failure. "Step 1: Stop work. Step 2: Notify supervisor. Step 3: Barricade area." Adds significant register maintenance complexity but removes ambiguity at the critical moment.
C. Optional TARP — register supports it, organisations configure it if they want it, platform shows default notification response if TARP not defined.
Consult: Do target organisations already have TARPs documented? If yes, the platform should surface them. If no, the platform shouldn't try to create the capability — focus on notification first.
4. Control health score vs composite risk score
Two possible scoring outputs from verification data. Control health score: what % of critical controls for this work type / MUE are currently in a healthy state. Composite site risk score: combines control health, near-miss rate, atrophy signal, and incident history into a single number per site.
Options to discuss:
A. Control health score only — narrowly defensible, directly actionable, doesn't collapse multiple signals into one number that becomes a management target rather than a signal.
B. Composite risk score — more powerful for leadership dashboards and board reporting. Risk: organisations optimise the score rather than the underlying conditions. Needs careful design to avoid becoming a vanity metric.
C. Both scores, kept visually separate — safety manager sees them side by side, not combined. Preserves the distinction between "are controls in place" and "is the site at risk."
Consult: What does leadership currently use to communicate site risk? If they already have a risk matrix or heat map, the platform needs to connect to that mental model. If they don't, you have the opportunity to define it — carefully.
5. Failed controls in incident investigations — tally and effectiveness
When an investigation is opened, the investigator can select which critical controls were absent or degraded as contributing factors. Tallying these across investigations over time produces control effectiveness data — a control that appears as a contributing factor in multiple investigations is structurally underperforming, not just occasionally absent.
Options to discuss:
A. Simple tally — count how many times each control appears as an investigation contributing factor. Surface in the register: "This control has appeared in 3 investigations in 6 months." Flag for register review.
B. Effectiveness signal — combine tally with verification pass rate. A control that verifies as in place 95% of the time but appears in 3 investigations suggests verification is not detecting real effectiveness. That gap is the insight.
C. Automatic register review trigger — when a control crosses a tally threshold, the register owner receives a notification to review the control definition, performance requirement, or verification method.
Consult: This is the most defensible use of the data and the clearest differentiator from existing CCM tools. Worth prioritising in the discovery conversations.
6. Register governance — who can change a control and how
The global register is pushed to sites. Local overrides are permitted within bounds. But who can add a control to the global register? Who approves changes? What happens to sites mid-verification-cycle when a control is updated?
Options to discuss:
A. Safety Manager role owns the register — they add, edit, and retire controls. Changes are versioned and pushed with a review step at site level.
B. Separate Register Owner role — a designated person (potentially above safety manager level) with exclusive write access to the global register. Prevents register drift from too many editors.
C. Change request model — anyone can propose a control change, Register Owner approves. Investigation corrective actions that target a control create a proposed amendment automatically.
Consult: How do target organisations currently manage their critical control registers? If they have no register today, you're creating the governance model as well as the tool — that's a significant change management ask.
